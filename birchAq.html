Birch Aquarium Exhibit Design

Affiliated with UC San Diego, the Birch Aquarium houses marine lives and offers various exhibits for visitors to enjoy. The exhibits are educational and interactive, designed to communicate the pressing environmental challenges of today. â€‹Current plans to remodel the exhibit focuses on the theme of exploration in science, as related to journeys taken by oceanographers to assess the recent changes in the natural environment. Diving frigid waters, exploring Antartica icebergs, and living in the middle of the Pacific Ocean are some of the adventurous and rewarding experiences that oceanographers encounter.

The Challenge

The Birch Aquarium hopes to portray scientists and oceanographers as explorers with the passion to better understand natural occurrences and preserve the Earth. They make week or even month-long trips to measure chemicals in the ocean water or study the coral changes. However, many portrayals of scientists through the media are often superficial and incomplete. Through stories, personal motivations, and challenges, this exhibit should aim to humanize scientists and challenge current stereotypes.


Goals

A well-designed exhibit should be engaging, family-friendly, and informative. After interacting with this exhibit, visitors should feel that they are able to relate to the scientists and understand the passion and motivation behind their work. Ultimately, we want the visitors to realize the importance of the work and be inspired to take part in protecting the Earth.

On-site observation and interview

Our team visited the Birch Aquarium to observe visitors' experience in the aquarium space. I wanted to find out which exhibits received a lot of attention and what aspects made them appealing.

Visual attraction Visitors loved the big floor-to-ceiling glass tank. Their eyes filled with wonder as they watched the school of fish zooming across the tank or the long, narrow seaweed flowing in the water. Kids pressed their noses and hands against the glass to get a look at the sea turtle swimming by. A 9 year-old told me: "Seeing the big fish was my favorite part!"

Physical interaction Kids gravitated towards any exhibit that had a button, lever, wheel, and so on. They were eager to interact with the exhibit and find out how it will respond. To them, it seems that the sense of surprise and novelty throughout the interaction made it a fun experience.

I also conduct interviews with 4 staff members (gift shop store manager, visitor service manager, guest greeter, and ticket collector) to learn about what experiences the visitors were walking away with. Since the staff members were very familiar with the aquarium, my goal was to find out what issues they can identify as needing improvement. I discovered a few points of interest:

1. Aquarium's message not clearly expressed May, the member greeter at the information desk, told me "Visitors were often more focused on the visual presentation rather than the overall message." She said that the aquarium is associated with the research institute and its goal is to promote engagement for environmental causes. In her point of view however, it's unclear whether visitors take that message to heart after leaving the aquarium.

2. Parents tend to not interact with the exhibits May commented about parents' lack of interaction: "Parents shy away from interacting with the exhibits. They think that the exhibits are geared towards children and they don't want to receive judgment for taking a child's spot." When adults are staying back and letting their kids play and explore, the aquarium loses an opportunity to communicate its message to a large portion of their visitors.

3. Outdated exhibit content A portion of the visitors are members or locals who frequently visit the aquarium. Nancy, the ticket collector, said that "members would like to see fresh content." Creating an exhibit that can be periodically updated can build excitement and incentivize the visitors to revisit.


I uncovered many good insights from the observation and interviews. Overall, I wanted to design an exhibit that is open to people of all ages and inspires visitors with messages about protecting the natural environment. These insights will frame the design process and later guide the prototype ideation.

Surveys

Going back to the exhibit goals of breaking the stereotypes of scientists, I wanted understand how people currently view scientists. I created a survey asking participants this question:
"What comes to mind when you imagine a scientist?"

Survey details: 58 participants   |   ages: 14-22   |   57.4% Female   37.7% Male   4.9% Other


6.9% of participants mentioned that scientists have a passion for making a difference and an interest in the living things and the environment. These descriptions portray scientists as intrinsically motivated and enthusiastic about their work.

51.7% of participants mentioned stereotypical characteristics such as wearing lab coats and using goggles and microscopes. These were general, physical descriptions that generalize what scientists do, but don't explain what their motivations might be.

INTERVIEWS

Insight In movies and shows, it's easy to see someone in white coats working in a lab with googles and call him or her a scientist. However, some people don't get a chance to know scientists on a personal level. This makes it difficult to learn more about them beyond the superficial stereotypes.

Speaking to a scientist or hearing his/her personal story about their interest in science can help people better understand personal drives that motivate their work as well as the challenges that come with it.

PROTOTYPING as A HYPOTHESIS

Knowing that personal stories can humanize scientists and make them more relatable, the design challenge was to incorporate this insight into a prototype. Although it would be ideal to invite scientists to the aquarium, it can be difficult to arrange the logistics. What if we could simulate a conversation with the scientists instead?

Design concept: We can conduct an interview with a scientist and ask them to discuss questions such as: "What motivates you to study in your field?", "What was one difficult challenge you encountered?", and "What impacts do you hope to make?" This gives them an opportunity to tell the untold stories about their passion and their work. With permission, we will videotape the interview and use it as part of the exhibit. A visitor can walk up to the digital exhibit, choose which scientist they'd like to learn about and ask them questions by selecting one of the displayed options. Then, they will be shown a video of the scientist personally answering that question.

Design Goal: Spark a connection and interest by hearing the first-hand experiences of a scientist. The stories that differ from the stereotypical images can expand the views of scientists, showing variety of topics, tools, and environments they work with. Furthermore, hearing the struggles and success brings out the imperfection that shows a more authentic perspective of the profession.

Prototype Testing

Do aquarium visitors see scientists in a new light and have a more well-rounded perspective of them? I went to the Birch Aquarium and asked interested visitors to test out the prototype. My goal was to get feedback on whether the exhibit prototype effectively addresses the design challenge. I received a wide range of responses from different user types.

Children: Children were easily distracted when watching the interview-style videos. They had difficulty paying attention to a video that shows a scientist talking to an interviewer behind the camera. Some kids didn't fully understand what was being said.

Teenagers: Teenagers were interested by the stories. They listened attentively and were eager to ask more questions. They told me that they learned novel and unexpected things about scientists. "It was nice to hear the scientist in her own voice, " one said.

Parents of kids: Since the kids were easily distractible, the adults were attending to their kids and had difficulty focusing on the prototype as well. We have to capture the kids' attention to even get a chance at catching the parents'.


Moving forward, we ideated on ways to improve the prototype. Looking back at the user research insights, the interactive component was present, but the visual component was lacking. The visual appeal might be a key to maintaining the attention and interested.

Final prototype

Can we use more engaging visuals to help direct the attention of young children? We iterated on the prototype by modifying the videos themselves. To engage younger audiences at the aquarium, we created an animation video that visually illustrates the stories being told. Drawings are sketched out and words are emphasized in correspondence with the pace of the story.

Credits to Emiliy Tao for the awesome video sketch!


In the final prototype, I incorporated the video sketch which offers a multimodal style of communication, engaging the audience through the story as well as the visuals. At the end of each video, users can select a question from the available choices to ask the scientist. Tapping on the question will lead to another video where the scientist will address the question, simulating a back-and-forth style of conversations.





Final Thoughts

The highlight of this project was the testing of the exhibit prototype in the real aquarium setting. With the help of aquarium goers who volunteered to try the prototype, I was able to see the prototype being used in the real context. Some interactions were anticipated while other reactions were insightful and informative of how the prototype can be modified to improve the experience. With just one visit to the aquarium, the exhibit design was improved and became much more human-centered.

By bringing out stories told authentically and enthusiastically by oceanographers, this exhibit design can help aquarium visitors understand oceanographers on a more personal basis. This essentially builds a sense of empathy and humanizes the oceanographers as scientists who are eager to explore the depths of the ocean as they seek to protect the nature. I am excited and proud to see this project come to completion, as it helps bring to light the aspects of passion, drive, and sacrifice that are often missing from portrayals of scientists.
